# The Vignette

The kettle had been on the blink for a fortnight. It would come to the boil and then forget it had done so, sitting there warm but silent, the switch still down, until you came back and found it had gone off the boil again. Margaret had learned to stand by it. She filled it to the line for two cups and waited with her hand near the switch.

Through the window over the sink she could see the back of the Pearsons' garage and the strip of grass between the fences that nobody owned and so nobody cut. It had rained in the night. The grass was flattened in one place where a cat or a fox had lain.

The kettle clicked. She poured the water onto the two bags and pressed them against the side of the mugs with the back of a spoon, first one and then the other, and dropped the bags in the caddy on the windowsill where they would sit until she remembered to empty it.

"Is there sugar in this," Roy said from the front room.

"Same as always," she said.

"It tastes like there's no sugar in it."

She brought the mugs through. He was in the chair by the window with the paper folded to the crossword, though he had not written anything in it. The pen was on the arm of the chair. He took the mug without looking at it and set it on the little table where it would leave a ring because he never used the coaster, and she had stopped putting the coaster out because it only meant she had to move it.

"Sixteen across," he said. "Six letters. Coming together at a point."

"I don't know."

"Begins with a V."

"I don't know, Roy."

He looked at the grid for a while. Then he picked up the pen and put it down again. The tea was too hot to drink and they both knew it and they both held their mugs anyway, for the warmth of them, because the heating did not come on until four.

On the mantelpiece there was a photograph of their daughter on her wedding day and beside it a smaller one of the grandchildren, who were not children any more. Karen had said she would ring on Sunday and it was Tuesday now. Margaret had not minded the first day. By Tuesday she had begun to compose, without meaning to, the things she would not say when Karen did ring — that it was no trouble, that they understood, that everyone was busy. She would say all of that and mean it, and the other thing, the small flat weight of it, would stay where it was.

"You could try the boiler man again," she said. "About the kettle. He's coming for the boiler."

"It's not worth his while for a kettle."

"You could ask. While he's here."

"We'll get a new one. They're nothing now. You can get one for a tenner."

"This one was forty."

"This one's broken," Roy said, not unkindly, and drank some of his tea and made a face because it was still too hot.

She sat on the settee with the mug on her knee. The room held the particular quiet of the afternoon, the clock on the wall and the fridge starting up in the kitchen and, a long way off, a car changing gear on the hill. She thought about whether to do the ironing now or after they had eaten. There was not much of it. Two of his shirts and the pillowcases. If she did it now she could watch the thing at six.

"Vertex," Roy said.

"What?"

"Sixteen across. Vertex. Coming together at a point." He wrote it in, the letters small and pressed hard so the nib nearly went through. "I knew it had a V."

He drank his tea. The ring it left on the table when he lifted it had spread a little at the edge. Margaret looked at it and decided she would wipe it when she got up, and went on sitting, because she was not ready to get up yet, and the tea in her own mug was cooling to the temperature where she would be able to drink it, and outside the light had not changed but the afternoon had gone on a little, the way it did.
