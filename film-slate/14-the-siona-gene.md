# FILM TREATMENT — The Siona Gene

> **Logline:** A queen whose perfect foresight has turned her kingdom into beautiful, deathless clockwork discovers a child she cannot see — and spends her last years secretly breeding blindness back into her people, burying her own gift as the rarest gift of all: a future no one can foresee.
> **Central recurring image:** A vast grey panorama of the kingdom rendered in flawless, frozen detail — and at its centre, a single snow-bright, person-shaped hole in the picture that the eye keeps falling into, the one place the future is still alive.
> **Runtime:** 30+ min · **Strand:** The Book of Gaps (Parable)

## Synopsis

In a kingdom ruled by a queen who sees every future in perfect, fixed colour, nothing fails and nothing surprises — harvests are stored before droughts, ambushes are turned aside before they spring, marriages are matched by their foreseen contentment. The people call it the Age of the Open Eye and they are grateful and safe. But the queen, alone in her grey tower, is dying of knowing: she has lived every moment twice, never been met by a single hour of her life, and has come to understand the secret horror of her gift — that a future you can see is a future already dead, that her looking does not reveal the not-yet but *kills* it. Then, in a cold northern valley, a child is born whom she cannot see — a living dark, a person-shaped hole in her omniscience. She weeps, having forgotten the name of the feeling, which is hope. In her last years she does the opposite of everything her gift ever did: she protects the child's opacity, lets the unforeseeable grow and fail and grieve unsoftened, and quietly breeds the small dark thing in the child's blood into the wide future of her kingdom — sowing blindness, making it untraceable. She dies in the grey foreseen way. The kingdom that follows is unsafe, unfortunate by every measure of the Open Eye — and, for the first time in living memory, alive. They never know what she gave them. That is the form the gift had to take.

## Movements

**I. The Age of the Open Eye** *(0:00–8:00)*
We SEE the kingdom at the height of its perfection: golden but bloodless, every field, market and marriage moving with the smoothness of a thing already rehearsed a thousand times. The queen on her throne names a newborn's whole life in a single calm breath; she turns a marching column aside before the ambush; granaries fill against a drought no cloud yet foretells. The people bow in gratitude. We linger on the queen's face — composed, exhausted, registering nothing, because nothing arrives that she has not already worn smooth. **TURN:** in the privacy of her tower the mask drops, and we feel that this flawless world is not a paradise but an embalming — every face she meets is a guest she has entertained ten thousand times.

**II. Dying of Knowing** *(8:00–15:00)*
The interior movement. We SEE the queen relive her own foreseen memories laid over the dull present — the same gesture, the same word, doubled, the live moment always arriving second-hand and grey. She tries not to see and cannot; the eye, once open, will not close. She traces her own foreseen death, far off and exact and exactly as grey as everything else. **TURN:** the recognition lands with quiet violence — *her seeing does not predict the future, it murders it in advance.* She has made the whole kingdom safe by making the whole kingdom dead, lovingly converting every living not-yet into clockwork she has already watched run down. The Age of the Open Eye is revealed, from inside, as a mausoleum she built with love.

**III. The Hole in the Future** *(15:00–24:00)*
A winter in the far north. The queen feels it the way you feel a tooth gone missing — a sudden soft blankness in her seeing. She turns her gaze on the cold valley and finds *nothing*: not death, not absence, but a living dark, a person-shaped hole, opaque and closed to her. For the first time in forty years she does not know what happens next. She weeps — the court thinks from fear; we know it is the return of a forgotten feeling. She journeys, an old woman now, to the valley and looks into a face she cannot read and feels her grey heart turn over. **TURN:** she resolves on the last and greatest act of her reign — to do the opposite of ruling. She will not foresee the child, will not guard or soften or match. She will *protect the not-knowing*, keep the court's eyes away, let the unforeseeable ones grow and fail and surprise her — and the surprise is the most alive she has been since girlhood.

**IV. Sowing Blindness** *(24:00–30:00+)*
The secret work of her last years. In candlelit privacy we SEE her find, in the child's blood, the small dark thing that closes them to her seeing — and breed it, valley to valley, line to line, into the wide future of the kingdom. She sows blindness on purpose. She makes it untraceable, because that is the whole point. She dies in the foreseen year, the grey foreseen way — the last thing she would ever see. The kingdom that comes after is unsafe: harvests fail, wars are lost, griefs go unsoftened, no one can say what tomorrow holds. **TURN — the emotional payload:** by every measure of the Open Eye they are worse off, and they are, for the first time in living memory, *alive* — leaning into days they cannot see the bottom of, met at every moment by a future no one owns. They never know they were given anything; a seen gift would have been a fixed gift, and a fixed gift is dead. She had loved them enough to make herself blind to them. That is the most a seer can do.

## Visual Vocabulary

**Palette — the central duality.** Omniscience is *grey*: not black, not bleak-monochrome, but the specific deadness of a perfect colour photograph left in the sun until the life leached out — desaturated golds, silvered greens, skies the colour of certainty. Everything the queen sees carries a faint over-rendered, lacquered finish, too sharp, too finished, as if already a memory. Against this, the unforeseeable arrives *snow-bright*: a high-key, living white that the grey cannot reach into, blizzard-luminous, slightly overexposed at its edges, holding the only saturated colour in the film (a child's flushed cheek, breath in cold air, a single living red).

**Motifs & textures.** The open eye and its lid. Clockwork — gears, orreries, granary mechanisms turning with eerie smoothness, the kingdom as a wound machine. Frost ferns and snow as the texture of the not-yet. The tower window as a frame the queen looks *out* of and is trapped *behind*. A loom or bloodline-diagram for the final sowing — lines branching into untraceable dark. The person-shaped hole itself: a deliberate absence in otherwise complete compositions.

**Image-system.** Foresight shown as the present overlaid with its own ghost — the same action faintly doubled, the live frame always lagging the rehearsed one. The hole in the future as a region of the frame that resists the grey's lacquer: softer, brighter, unresolvable. As the film ends, the snow-bright begins to bleed outward into the grey kingdom — blindness spreading as light.

**Transitions.** Match-cuts between foreseen and arrived versions of the same moment (the grey fades up underneath the live one). Hard cuts from the lacquered grey court to the raw blizzard-white valley. A recurring slow push *into* the person-shaped hole until the screen goes living white.

## Sound & Score

A tonal world of two textures. Omniscience SOUNDS like a held drone, glassine and pitch-perfect, with a clockwork pulse beneath — strings bowed without vibrato, a music that has nowhere to go because it already knows its last note. The unforeseeable breaks this open: sudden room-tone, wind, breath, a single unresolved interval, the *imperfection* of live sound — a held note that wavers, a heartbeat that is not on the grid. Silence is the queen's true register; the tower scenes run on near-silence so the doubling of foreseen-over-arrived can be felt. Narration is *sparse* — only the parable's hardest turns spoken in a low, weathered, ungrieved voice, never explaining, never more than a line at a time: *a future you can see is a future that has already happened* · *she was burying her own gift, on purpose, as a gift* · *that is the most a seer can do.* The final movement should run as wordless as possible — the spreading snow-bright carries the meaning, not speech.

## Tone & Touchstones

Elegiac, austere, tender beneath ice — a tragedy of love expressed as deliberate self-blinding, never sentimental, never explained. The grief is structural, not emotional weeping; the warmth arrives only as light. **Touchstones:** Borges's "Funes the Memorious" (the curse of total, lossless knowing) crossed with the visual austerity of late Tarkovsky and the cold-mythic stillness of *The Tree of Life*'s wordless passages; a fairy-tale gravity in the key of Le Guin, where the moral is a structure of fate rather than a lesson.

## Generation Notes

- **Emphasise** the over-rendered, lacquered, slightly-too-finished quality of the grey omniscient world — perfection as embalming, beauty that reads as death. The viewer should feel the smoothness as wrong.
- **The one image to nail:** the snow-bright, person-shaped hole in the otherwise complete grey picture of the kingdom — a living absence the eye keeps falling into. Everything in the film should orbit this single shot.
- **Emphasise** the doubling of foreseen-over-arrived: the present moment always shadowed by its own grey ghost arriving a beat earlier.
- **Emphasise** the spreading of light at the end — snow-bright bleeding outward into the grey, blindness sowing itself as luminance across the kingdom.
- **AVOID** any text-on-screen explaining the premise; no essay narration; no lecture about prophecy or free will. Let the grey-versus-snow-bright contrast and the person-shaped hole carry the idea wordlessly.
- **AVOID** over-explaining the queen's gift mechanically — keep it mythic and felt, shown through faces and overlays, never diagrammed.

# The Siona Gene

---

There was once a woman who could see what would happen.

Not vaguely. Not in dreams or omens. She saw the future the way you see the room you are sitting in — plainly, in full colour, every detail fixed. When a child was born, she saw the whole of its life at once: the loves, the betrayals, the last breath and the hour of it. When two armies marched toward each other, she saw the field of the dead before the first sword was drawn.

The people made her their queen, of course. Who would not be ruled by one who could not be surprised? Under her seeing, the kingdom became perfect. No harvest failed, because she saw the drought coming and they stored against it. No war was lost, because she saw the ambush and turned the column aside. No marriage soured, because she matched only those she had seen grow old together, content. There was no crime, no famine, no waste, no grief that had not been foreseen and softened. The people called it the Age of the Open Eye, and they were grateful, and they were safe.

And the queen, alone in her tower, was dying of something none of them could see, because they could not see anything at all.

She was dying of *knowing*.

---

It had crept up on her over the years, and she had not named it until it was nearly complete. It was this: nothing could happen to her that had not already happened. She lived every moment twice — once in the foreseeing, years before, and once in the dull arrival of the thing she had already worn smooth with looking. She had not been surprised since she was a girl. She had not laughed at anything she did not expect, because she expected everything. She had not been *met* by a single moment of her life. Each one came to her like a guest she had already entertained a thousand times, until she was sick to death of her own future and could not die of it because she had already seen, with weary precision, the year and the manner of her death, which was far away, and certain, and exactly as grey as everything else.

And she understood, slowly, the thing the seeing had hidden from her by being so useful: *a future you can see is a future that has already happened.* The looking fixed it. Her gift did not reveal the future — it *killed* it, in advance, turned the living not-yet into one more dead and finished thing, a corpse she carried around in her eyes for years before the world caught up to it. She had made the whole kingdom safe by making the whole kingdom dead. Nothing was alive in it, because nothing in it could surprise her, and she had begun to suspect — alone, in the tower, in the grey — that to be alive *was* to be able to surprise, that the not-yet was the whole of what made a thing a thing and not a mechanism, and that she had spent her reign lovingly converting every living future in her kingdom into clockwork she had already watched run down.

She could not stop seeing. She had tried. You cannot un-know. The eye, once open, does not close.

But there was, she discovered, one thing she could do.

---

In the far north of the kingdom, in a cold valley she rarely looked at, a child was born one winter whom the queen could not see.

She felt it the way you feel a tooth gone missing — a sudden soft blankness where there had always been bone. She turned her seeing on the child, and there was *nothing there*: not death, not absence, but a living dark, a hole in the future shaped like a person, opaque, unforeseeable, *closed to her.* For the first time in forty years the queen did not know what would happen next.

She wept. The court thought she wept from fear. She wept from something she had forgotten the name of, which was *hope* — the specific aliveness of standing at the edge of a moment you have not already buried.

She went to the cold valley herself, an old woman now, and found the child, and looked into a face she could not read, and felt her grey heart turn over. And she understood what she had to do, and that it would be the last and greatest act of her reign, and that no one would ever thank her for it, because no one would ever know.

She did not rule the child. She did not foresee the child's marriages or guard the child's harvests or soften the child's griefs. She did the opposite of everything her gift had ever done. She *protected the not-knowing.* She kept the court's eyes away from the cold valley. She let the unforeseeable ones grow, and marry whom they chose, and fail, and grieve unsoftened, and surprise her — and the surprise was the most alive she had been since she was a girl, and she guarded their opacity the way she had once guarded the kingdom's safety, because she had finally learned which one was worth the keeping.

And in her last years she did the secret work. She found, among the unforeseeable child's blood, the small dark thing that closed them to her seeing — and she bred it, quietly, patiently, valley to valley, line to line, into the wide future of the kingdom. She sowed blindness. She planted, in the soil of her own all-seeing reign, the seed of a people she would not be able to see, a people whose futures would stay *future*, alive and unfinished and free, opaque even to the open eye, surprising even to a god.

She was burying her own gift, on purpose, as a gift.

---

The queen died in the foreseen year, in the grey foreseen way, which was the last thing in the kingdom she had ever been able to see. By then the cold valley's blood had spread further than even she could trace, which was the whole point — she had made it untraceable.

The Age of the Open Eye ended with her, and was not mourned, and was barely remembered. The kingdom that came after was not safe. Harvests failed. Wars were lost. Marriages soured and griefs went unsoftened and no one could tell you what tomorrow held.

The people of that kingdom were, by every measure of the Open Eye, worse off.

They were also, for the first time in living memory, *alive* — leaning into days they could not see the bottom of, met at every moment by a future no one owned, surprising and being surprised, which they had forgotten was the rarest and most expensive thing a queen had ever given away.

They did not know they had been given anything. That was the form the gift had to take. A seen gift would have been a fixed gift, and a fixed gift is a dead one.

She had loved them enough to make herself blind to them.

That is the most a seer can do.
