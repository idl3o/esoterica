# FILM TREATMENT — The Scattering

> **Logline:** An immortal king who has guarded the world's last seed behind seven doors for a thousand years learns that the only way to keep it is to lose it, and walks out into a bare field to bury it and never look back.
> **Central recurring image:** A weathered hand pressing a single pale seed into dark, open earth — the same gesture returned to in the vault (refused) and the field (completed), and finally inverted as roots take a body up into a tree.
> **Runtime:** 30+ min · **Strand:** The Book of Gaps (Parable)

## Synopsis

In a palace lit by torchlight and tallow, an ancient king descends nightly through seven doors to a vault where a single seed — the last of the world-tree — sleeps in a box of carved bone. For a thousand years he has lost nothing, and this is his pride. The seed has never sprouted, because seeds in boxes do not sprout. One night a gardener so old her hands look like roots asks, plainly, to see it — and pronounces it dead precisely because it is whole. What follows is the slow unmaking of a man: the recognition that a seed is a promise, that a promise kept in a box is a promise broken, that splitting open in the dark is not death but beginning. At dawn he carries the bone box out past his own shrinking palace to a wide field of plain earth, kneels, and pushes the seed into the soil with his thumb. Then he does the harder thing — he turns and walks away without marking the spot, scattering the palace, the doors, the empty box, and at last himself, until a forest no one can trace grows on the far side of the world, where the people have never heard of any king and have never been taught that to be scattered is to be lost.

## Movements

**I. The Keeping** *(0:00–9:00)*
We SEE the architecture of holding: torchlight crawling down stone stairs, seven doors unlocking in sequence, a thousand small rituals of the lock and the lamp. The king's hands, very old, lifting the lid of a bone box to look at a seed he has looked at ten thousand times. Time compresses — the same descent repeated across seasons we glimpse only through the high window's changing light, while below nothing changes at all. The vault is immaculate, sealed, eternal. We feel the pride of *nothing taken* curdle, slowly, into the airlessness of a tomb. **The TURN:** the king mouths, alone in the dark, *I have lost nothing* — and the camera holds on the seed until its perfect wholeness begins to look like a held breath that will never be released.

**II. The Gardener** *(9:00–18:00)*
We SEE a door opened upward for once — an old woman admitted because she seems to want nothing, her root-hands, her unhurried gaze. The two of them descend together; for the first time another set of eyes is in the vault. She looks at the seed for a long, unbearable time and says it is dead, and *that is how she knows*. The film lets the dialogue breathe in near-darkness, faces half-lit. She names the thing gardeners know and kings forget: the box is the death, not the burying; the splitting open is not loss but the beginning that perfect keeping has forbidden. **The TURN:** the king says, "Then I will not be in it." She answers, "You will be *all* of it — but not anywhere it could be kept." We see the words land in his face like soil closing over something. The emotional payload here is grief that has not yet learned it is also release.

**III. The Field** *(18:00–26:00)*
Morning. We SEE the king walk out of the palace alone, the bone box in his hands, the people watching, not understanding. The palace shrinks behind him until it is small in front of nothing. He reaches a wide field of plain dark earth where wind moves the grass freely — the most open image in the film after the most enclosed. The seed lies in his palm: last of its kind, perfect, whole, *his*. The single decisive action of the whole film: he kneels, pushes the seed into the soil with his thumb, covers it, presses the earth flat, stands, brushes off his hands. **The TURN — and the heart of the film:** he turns and walks away. He does not mark the spot. He builds no fence, posts no guard, does not return at dawn to see if it sprouted. The camera stays on the unmarked ground as his footsteps recede; the act of *not looking back* is staged as the bravest thing a man can do.

**IV. The Forest** *(26:00–32:00+)*
We SEE the scattering complete itself beyond him: the palace given away, the seven doors standing open, the empty bone box left in grass, the king walking out as a wanderer with nothing in his hands, telling the story to strangers who plant other seeds in other fields and forget where they heard it. Then a leap across centuries and oceans. A forest, on the far side of the world from where he died, full of light and fruit. People who have never heard of any king eat fruit that contains all fruits, plant seeds they do not guard, and bury their dead under young trees. **The TURN / payload:** we watch roots take a body up into leaf and fruit and the seeds birds carry off — the burying gesture from Movement III inverted into rising — and no one finds it sad, because they were never taught that to be scattered is to be lost. The final image is the canopy moving in wind: the seed, completed, in a place that cannot keep it.

## Visual Vocabulary

A hard tonal split governs the palette. The first half lives in **vault chiaroscuro** — bone-white seed, amber torchlight, cold blue-black stone, deep crushed shadow, everything sealed and reflective and still. The second half opens into **field and forest light** — wide skies, wind-moved grass, loam browns, green broken by sun, an airiness that feels almost physically like exhaling. Recurring motifs: the seven doors (locks, hinges, keys) versus the single open palm; the carved bone box as a tiny coffin; root-textures echoed from the gardener's hands to the soil to the final canopy. The image-system rhymes three hands across three soils — refusing in the vault, burying in the field, dissolving into roots in the forest. Transitions favor slow dissolves and match-cuts on the burying gesture: thumb-into-box dissolving to thumb-into-earth dissolving to root-into-flesh. The film tightens to claustrophobic framing in the vault and progressively widens until the final shots have no walls at all.

## Sound & Score

The vault is a world of close, dry sound — the scrape of bone on bone, locks, a single guttering flame, the king's own breath amplified by stone. Near-silence carries the gardener's verdict; let the long pauses do the work. As the film moves outward, sound opens: wind, grass, distant birds, eventually the layered living hum of a forest. Score is sparse and low — a single sustained drone or cello tone under the vault, slowly admitting more instruments and air as the world opens, resolving in the forest into something warm, modal, almost a folk melody half-remembered. The film is **largely wordless**; the only spoken language is the gardener's exchange with the king, kept intimate and unhurried. The closing saying — *What you keep, you bury. What you scatter, you become.* — should arrive not as on-screen text but, if at all, as a single quiet voice over the canopy, or simply be carried by the image and left unspoken.

## Tone & Touchstones

Mood: hushed, reverent, slow, ultimately liberating — a fable told at the pace of soil. It should feel like a held breath finally released. Touchstones: the parable-stillness and natural light of Tarkovsky (the seed and the candle of *Nostalghia*, the levitating *Mirror*); the wordless mythic compression of a Studio Ghibli forest sequence; the patient long-take elegies of *The Tree of Life*. Above all it should feel like a thing grown rather than written.

## Generation Notes

- **Emphasise** the tonal arc from sealed vault-dark to open windblown light — the whole meaning lives in that opening-out; never let the second half look as enclosed as the first.
- **The one image to nail:** the weathered thumb pressing the single pale seed into dark open earth, then the hand withdrawing — shot so the *withdrawal* and the *not marking the spot* read as courage, not loss.
- Render the gardener's root-hands and the final root-into-body so the audience feels the rhyme without a word explaining it.
- Keep the king's face the instrument of the turn: pride, then airlessness, then grief, then a release he doesn't have words for.
- **AVOID:** any on-screen text lecturing the theme; essay-style or explanatory narration; over-literal CGI "magic forest." Trust silence, light, and the single gesture.

# The Scattering

---

There was once a king who owned a single seed.

It was the last of its kind. The tree it had come from was the only tree that had ever borne it, and that tree was long dead, and the king had spent his life guarding this one seed in a box of carved bone, in a vault beneath the palace, behind seven doors.

He guarded it well. No one had ever seen it. No thief had ever reached it. In a thousand years of keeping, the king had lost nothing — and this was his great pride. *I have held it perfectly,* he would say to himself in the dark of the vault. *Not one thing has been taken from me.*

The seed did not sprout. Seeds in boxes do not sprout. The king knew this, distantly, the way you know a thing you have decided not to think about. He told himself he was waiting for the right soil, the right season, the right hands. None ever came that were good enough. So he kept it. And kept it. And the keeping became the whole of his life, until he could no longer remember whether he was guarding the seed or the seed was guarding him.

One night an old woman came to the palace. She was a gardener, or had been; her hands looked like roots. She asked to see the king, and because she was very old and seemed to want nothing, they let her in.

"I have heard," she said, "that you possess the last seed of the world-tree."

"I do," said the king. "It is perfectly safe."

"Show me."

And because no one had ever asked plainly, and because he was old too, and tired, the king led her down through the seven doors, into the vault, and opened the box of carved bone.

The old woman looked at the seed for a long time. Then she said: "It is dead."

"It is *not* dead," said the king. "It is whole. It is exactly as it was. I have lost nothing."

"Yes," she said gently. "That is how I know it is dead."

---

The king did not understand, and the old woman saw that he did not, and so she told him the thing that gardeners know and kings forget.

"A seed is not a treasure," she said. "A seed is a *promise*, and a promise kept in a box is a promise broken. This seed was never meant to stay a seed. It was meant to be buried — to be *lost* in the dark of the soil, to split open, to stop being a seed entirely. That splitting is not its death. The box is its death. You have kept it so perfectly that you have never let it begin."

"But if I bury it, I lose it," said the king. "It will be gone. I will have nothing."

"You already have nothing," she said. "You have a dead thing in a box. If you bury it, you will have *everything* — but you will not be able to hold it, and you will not be able to keep it, and you will not be able to see most of it, ever. It will become a forest in lands you will never walk. Birds will carry its seeds to islands you will never name. In a thousand years there will be world-trees on every shore, and not one of them will know your name, and *that* will be the seed, completed."

The king was quiet for a long while in the dark of the vault.

"Then I will not be in it," he said at last.

"No," said the old woman. "You will be *all* of it. But you will not be anywhere it could be kept."

---

In the morning the king walked out of the palace alone, carrying the box of carved bone, and the people watched him go and did not understand.

He walked until the palace was small behind him, and then small in front of nothing, and he came to a wide field of plain dark earth where the wind moved freely. He opened the box. The seed lay in his palm, last of its kind, perfect, whole, his.

He thought about the seven doors. He thought about the thousand years. He thought about how it felt to have lost nothing, and he understood, finally, that he had lost everything by losing nothing, that a thing held closely enough is a thing already gone.

He knelt and pushed the seed into the soil with his thumb, and covered it, and pressed the earth flat, and stood, and brushed off his hands.

And then — because the old woman had told him this part too, and he had not believed her until now — he did the harder thing. He turned and walked away, and did not mark the spot, and did not build a fence around it, and did not post a guard, and did not return the next day to see whether it had sprouted. He let it go into the dark and the not-knowing, into the rain and the worms and the long slow work that needs no king.

He scattered, as well, the only other thing he had left to scatter, which was himself: he gave away the palace and the seven doors and the empty bone box, and went out as a wanderer with nothing in his hands, and told the story of the world-tree to anyone who would listen, and some of them planted trees of their own, of other kinds, in other fields, and forgot where they had heard the story, and that forgetting was the seed too.

---

He never saw the tree.

There is a forest there now, on the far side of the world from where he died, and the people who live in it have never heard of any king. They eat the fruit, which contains all fruits. They plant the seeds, which they do not guard. When one of them dies, they bury him under a young tree, and the tree takes him up into leaf and fruit and the seeds the birds carry off, and no one finds this sad, because they have never been taught that to be scattered is to be lost.

They have a saying, in that forest, that no one remembers the origin of.

*What you keep, you bury. What you scatter, you become.*
