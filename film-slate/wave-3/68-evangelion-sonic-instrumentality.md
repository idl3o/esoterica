# FILM TREATMENT — Evangelion — Sonic Instrumentality

> **Logline:** When every separate voice on Earth begins to dissolve into one painless, perfect tone, a single boy must decide whether to let the chord resolve forever — or break it back into the bruised, dissonant, gloriously alive chorus of selves who can still hurt each other.
> **Central recurring image:** A vast orange sea under a fractured sky, its surface trembling — and one cross-shaped silhouette of a boy refusing to lie down flat into it.
> **Runtime:** 30+ min · **Strand:** The Other Fictions

## Synopsis
We open inside a sound, not a place: a manic baroque rush, all peak and no rest, racing over images a child should never have seen. The film is built as an argument made entirely of sound. A boy — never named on screen, only breathing, only repeating *I mustn't run away* until the words go dead in his mouth — pilots something enormous against things that eat people, and the heroic music will not stop, will not let him feel. Then the music begins to fail. Sacred classical pieces bleed into scenes of violation and dissolution; beauty refuses to sanctify anything. The score thins to a single sustained synthesizer note, cicadas, a train going nowhere, until isolation becomes unbearable and dissolution starts to sound like mercy. Then: total silence. Every individual consciousness on Earth pours out as orange fluid, every separate voice folding toward one held tone — Ode to Joy warped into a lullaby of ending. In the silence where his self used to be, the boy is offered painlessness: merge, and never be alone again. He almost takes it. Then he hears, faintly, the others — not as one tone but as discord, as friction, as the people who could wound him because they are *really other*. He chooses the dissonance. The chord refuses to resolve. He un-merges back into a world that can hurt, because being able to be hurt is being. A nostalgic jazz standard plays over the wreckage, lying gently. The sea is still there. He is standing.

## Movements
### Movement I — The Manic Defense (0:00–8:00)
We never see a clear face. We see fragments — a hand gripping a control yoke, the back of a child's neck, the wet sheen of an eye — all of it driven forward by a 145-BPM baroque triumph that has no build and no release, only sustained climax. Strings, brass, layered vocals stacked into cognitive overload. Over this manic engine: giant beings that devour people, a city evacuating, a child strapped into a war machine. The music insists everything is glorious. The cutting rhymes to the tempo: run, fight, run, don't feel. We feel the relief of it — battle as escape from consciousness, motion mistaken for meaning. Lips form *I mustn't run away* over and over until repetition empties the words. **The TURN:** mid-crescendo, the orchestra drops out for half a beat — and in that gap we hear a single child breathing, terrified, and realize the triumphant music was never the truth. It was the lie protecting us from the breath.

### Movement II — Beauty Will Not Save You (8:00–16:00)
Now the sacred music arrives — Bach, Handel, a Pachelbel canon, the cathedral repertoire of weddings and transcendence. But it plays over the wrong images: children killing and dying, a tenderness curdling into violation, elevation laid like gold leaf over horror. The contrast is not ironic; it is corrosive. We watch beauty fail to sanctify, fail to protect, fail to mean goodness. The frequencies of the holy and the profane are shown to be the *same frequency*. A choir swells exactly as something unforgivable happens. The film teaches the eye and ear together: the categories you trusted to stay apart can occupy the same space. **The TURN:** Ode to Joy begins — the ultimate music of human fellowship — and as it rises we see the first AT-fields, the membranes between selves, beginning to soften and weep. If beauty and horror share one tone, then so can self and other. The merge has already begun, and it is wearing the most beautiful music ever written.

### Movement III — The Sea of One Tone (16:00–25:00)
The score thins. A single sustained synth note holds and holds. Cicadas. A train. Breathing in an empty room. Isolation becomes physical, time distends, meaning drains out as sound. Then the dissolution proper: human shapes lose their edges and pour out as orange LCL, surface tension breaking, every separate person collapsing toward the great sea. On the soundtrack, the many voices of humanity — billions of distinct tones — slide microtone by microtone into unison, the dissonance smoothing into one impossibly pure, painless chord. No more friction. No more hedgehog's dilemma, no more wounding by getting close. The boy floats at the center, his own edges going soft, and the held tone promises: lie flat, and never be lonely again. It sounds exactly like salvation. **The TURN:** total silence. The chord completes — and in completion there is no one left to hear it. The self that wanted peace is gone. Silence is not death; death has screaming. This is the soundlessness of there being no one to make a sound.

### Movement IV — The Refusal That Keeps the Others Real (25:00–32:00+)
From inside the silence, the boy chooses to return. We hear it begin as a single rough, off-pitch sound — his own voice, alone, imperfect — and it is the most alive thing in the film precisely because it does *not* match the perfect tone. He reconstitutes: a name, a boundary, a refusal. The single chord cracks back apart into a chorus that is dissonant, abrasive, unresolved — the sound of people who are really other, who can therefore really hurt him, and whom he chooses anyway. The AT-fields re-form like skin over a wound. The merge is the temptation of dissolved unity; the un-merge is the keeping of otherness, and the bodhisattva-grade fact is that the vow only has teeth if the others stay *really* other. The orange sea recedes. A nostalgic jazz standard rises over the ruined beach — gentle, lying, offering its little escape — and we let it, briefly. **The TURN:** the boy is standing, breathing, alone-and-not-alone, and the final sound is not the perfect tone and not the lie of the love song. It is two voices, slightly out of tune with each other, continuing.

## Visual Vocabulary
**Palette:** LCL orange and clinical white as the two poles — the warm drowning sea against the cold antiseptic of separateness; bruised reds and blacks for the manic battles; bleached summer light, faded photograph tones, for the isolation passages.
**Motifs/textures:** the cross-shaped silhouette; surface tension breaking on orange fluid; the back of a neck; a single eye in extreme close-up; membranes/skins softening and re-forming; the held vertical of a standing figure refusing the horizontal of the sea.
**Image-system:** edges as the whole argument made visible — sharp boundaries (the self) dissolving into edgeless field (the merge) and then re-crisping into hard outline. Faces fragment and reassemble.
**Transitions:** match-cuts on a single sustained note; hard cuts to total black-silence at the dissolution peaks; the merge rendered as a slow optical bleed where two separate frames overlay into one.

## Sound & Score
**Tonal world:** the entire film is a sonic thesis — the design IS the meaning. The arc moves deliberately: many distinct voices → manic overlayered density → sacred beauty weaponized over horror → thinning to one held tone → the perfect painless unison → total silence → one rough imperfect voice → the return of living dissonance.
**Music:** manic baroque (no release, all peak) for Movement I; sacred classical recontextualized over horror for II; a single sustained synthesizer tone, cicadas, train hum, breath for III's isolation; a warped Ode-to-Joy gliding into perfect unison at the merge; pure silence at the apex; then a deliberately off-pitch solo human voice cracking the chord; a nostalgic mid-century jazz standard, lying gently, over the close.
**Narration:** near-wordless. Only the breath, and the emptied mantra heard once early — *I mustn't run away* — repeated until it loses meaning, then never spoken again. The choice is carried by tone, not by speech.

## Tone & Touchstones
*Under the Skin* (Glazer) for the sentient sea and the body dissolving into field; *2001: A Space Odyssey* for classical music over the cosmically overwhelming; *Stalker* (Tarkovsky) for the unbearable patient isolation soundscape; *The Tree of Life* for sacred music laid over both creation and grief.

## Generation Notes
- **Emphasise:** the SOUND is the spine — every visual choice should serve the sonic argument (many voices → one tone → the chosen return to dissonance). Build the merge as the most beautiful sound in the film so the refusal costs something.
- **The one image to nail:** the vast orange sea under a cracked sky with one cross-shaped boy refusing to lie flat into it — verticality against the horizontal of dissolution.
- **Make painlessness genuinely seductive:** the perfect unison must feel like mercy, not menace, or the choice means nothing.
- **The refusal is the engine, not a cost:** keeping the others *really* other — able to wound — is the whole point; the dissonant chorus at the end should feel like victory, not consolation.
- **Render silence as a presence, not an absence:** hold it long enough to become unbearable, so the single returning voice lands as a resurrection.
- **AVOID:** any text on screen, diagrams, essay-voiceover, or franchise logos/mecha iconography. No explaining. Let the sound make the argument; the boy never names what he is choosing.
