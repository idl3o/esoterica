# FILM TREATMENT — Being Mercury — A Day in the Life of the Messenger

> **Logline:** Across one unremarkable day — waking, texting, commuting, working, eating, sleeping — a single ordinary person keeps becoming the god of crossings, until the divide between errand and ritual dissolves and the wings turn out to have been on their feet the whole time.
> **Central recurring image:** A bead of mercury — liquid silver — rolling: across a thumb, down a windowpane, along a keyboard's edge, never stopping, always connecting one thing to the next.
> **Runtime:** 30+ min · **Strand:** The Self-Portrait

## Synopsis
A person wakes before the alarm. Eyes still shut, breath arriving like a courier from somewhere far. This film follows them through one entirely ordinary day and finds, woven into every threshold they cross, the oldest job in the cosmos: carrying messages between worlds. The phone on the nightstand becomes a caduceus; the unread messages, consciousness seeking connection. A commute becomes shamanic travel, red lights a pause for dimensional shifting. At work the desk is an altar, the keyboard a ritual tool, the meeting a sacred circle where one quiet person keeps translating the technical into the human and introducing the people who needed to meet. There is a difficult conversation survived through humor used as lubricant for a hard truth. There is a meal cooked — ingredients drawn from plant and animal and mineral realms, transformed by heat, bridged from kitchen to a shared table. The film never names a framework, never explains. It simply watches the divine glimpsed through the mundane: every text sent, every gap bridged, every boundary crossed. By evening the person reviews the day — what did I connect; what did I translate — and lays down to sleep knowing they will travel all realms again. The wings were never elsewhere.

## Movements
### Movement I — The Courier Arrives (0:00–8:00)
Darkness. The sound of breath. A close shot of closed eyelids, flickering — someone returning from somewhere. A hand reaches across a sheet before the eyes open: arriving. The body stretches, arms wide — for one frame the morning light through the curtain reads like the suggestion of wings opening at the shoulders. A bead of mercury rolls across a thumb and the person looks at it without surprise. On the nightstand the phone wakes; its screen-glow is the first message of the day, notifications stacking like birds landing on a wire. The thumb that held the silver now scrolls. We watch them reply to a text — a small thing, a heart, an "on my way" — and the camera lingers as the message lifts off the screen and is gone. Coffee. Steam crossing from cup to face. **The TURN:** they pause at the front door, hand on the handle, and we see — in the brass of the handle — the faint reflection of two small wings at their heels, there and then gone as the door opens onto the street.

### Movement II — The Crossings (8:00–16:00)
The day's traffic of thresholds. The commute: a train window streaked with rain, beads of water running down and joining, the city sliding past as separate worlds glimpsed and left. A red light; the person waits, perfectly still amid the crowd, and for a breath everyone else blurs while they hold steady — the still point in the moving. At the office the desk resolves into an altar without anyone saying so: monitor as scrying glass, mouse moving like a small wand, a cursor blinking. In a meeting they say little; the camera catches them leaning to translate one colleague's jargon into another's plain speech, watching the second face soften with understanding. They introduce two strangers by the coffee machine; a handshake; a connection made that will outlive the day. **The TURN:** mid-afternoon a difficult conversation — tense shoulders, a hard thing that must be said — and the person lands a small, warm joke; the other person laughs despite themselves, and the wall between them quietly comes down. The mercury bead rolls across the desk's edge and doesn't fall.

### Movement III — The Alchemy of the Ordinary (16:00–25:00)
Evening descends into the domestic. A grocery store: fluorescent aisles, a cashier handing back change, the exchange of money for crystallized possibility, a receipt like a small confirmation slip. Home. The kitchen becomes the day's most sacred room. Ingredients laid out — green herb, red meat, white salt, drawn from the three realms — and the hands begin to transform them: chopping that crosses one thing into many, heat that translates raw into cooked, a spoon that bridges pot to mouth for the taste. A child or a partner is handed a plate; the meal carried from counter to table is a message delivered. Laughter at the table; an inside joke that is a private sacred language between two people. A phone face-down now, deliberately — a small fast of silence. **The TURN:** in the quiet after the meal, the person stands at the sink and watches a single bead of dish-soap mercury-silver slide down the window into the dark garden — and they smile, recognizing the messenger in the most ordinary water on glass.

### Movement IV — Return to the Far Country (25:00–32:00+)
Twilight integration. The person sits at the edge of the bed in lamplight and looks back over the day — not in words, but in a montage of small returns: the morning text, the translated meeting, the handshake, the joke, the meal. Each remembered crossing flickers as a tiny gold thread laid across darkness, until the threads form a loose weave — a single day's web. They turn off the lamp. The body settles. We return to the breath, slowing now. The eyelids close. The bead of mercury, last seen on the window, rolls one final time — off the edge of frame, into sleep, into the realm the courier will travel tonight. The last image: bare feet at the end of the bed, relaxed, ordinary — and the faintest shimmer of wings, not added, simply revealed. Hold. Fade. The day will begin again tomorrow as the same ancient errand.

## Visual Vocabulary
**Palette:** Warm domestic naturalism — morning amber, office grey-blue, kitchen gold, twilight indigo — shot through with one cold recurring accent: the liquid silver of mercury and the bluish glow of screens. Skin tones true and close. Nothing heightened until the small reveals, which are kept to a glint, never a glow.
**Motifs/textures:** Liquid silver beading and rolling; water on glass joining and running; reflections in brass, screens, spoons, windows; hands — always hands, holding, typing, chopping, reaching; thresholds, doorways, traffic lights, checkout counters; faint wings at heels and shoulders, seen only in reflection or peripheral light.
**Image-system:** The day as a chain of crossings. Every scene is built around a boundary being passed — sleep to waking, inside to outside, stranger to stranger, raw to cooked, separation to connection. The mercury bead is the through-line stitching the scenes; it appears at least once per movement, always in motion, never falling or stopping.
**Transitions:** Match-cuts on round silver forms — coffee bead to rain-bead to soap-bead to the closing roll-off; cuts on the act of crossing a threshold (door, turnstile, doorway) so the film itself keeps stepping through.

## Sound & Score
**Tonal world:** Intimate and close-mic'd — the small sounds of a real day: the tick of a kettle, the soft concussion of a sent message, footsteps, the murmur of a meeting heard from inside one quiet head, the knife on the board, water on glass. Room tone as presence.
**Music:** Sparse and quicksilver — a single bright instrument (celesta, vibraphone, or muted glockenspiel) sketching a light, mercurial figure that recurs and reconfigures with each crossing, never resolving, always passing on. Warmer strings only at the table and the closing return to breath.
**Narration:** Near-wordless. No essay, no explanation, no framework named aloud. The only human words are the ambient, overheard fragments of the actual day — a "thank you," a joke half-caught, "on my way" — diegetic and unforced. The images carry the entire argument.

## Tone & Touchstones
*Paterson* (Jarmusch) for the sacred repetition of one ordinary day. *Wings of Desire* (Wenders) for the unseen messenger moving among unknowing people. *Perfect Days* (Wenders) for ritual found in routine and light on glass. *Jeanne Dielman* (Akerman) for the domestic gesture held long enough to become rite.

## Generation Notes
- **Emphasise:** the GROUNDED and the everyday — this is the intimate, human-scaled film of the slate; the divine is glimpsed through the mundane, never staged as the cosmic. Keep settings ordinary: a real bedroom, a real commute, a real kitchen.
- **The one image to nail:** the bead of liquid mercury rolling — across thumb, window, desk edge — never falling, always connecting one thing to the next. It must read as physical and real, not CGI-slick.
- **Keep the wings subliminal:** wings at heels and shoulders appear ONLY in reflection, peripheral light, or a single held frame — suggestion, never declaration. If a viewer isn't sure they saw it, that is correct.
- **Find the crossings:** build every scene around one boundary being passed (sleep/waking, inside/outside, stranger/stranger, raw/cooked, self/other). The structure is the argument.
- **AVOID:** any text on screen, any spoken explanation, any framework names, any diagram or invocation read aloud, any mythological costume or temple-set literalism. No god appears. Only a person, and the day, and the silver thread between things.
