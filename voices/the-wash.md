# The Wash

Her mother weighs eighty-eight pounds now and cannot turn herself over. So Carol turns her, every two hours through the night, because if she does not the skin breaks down over the sacrum and the heels, and a sore there goes to the bone and does not heal in a body that small.

The morning is the wash. She warms a basin of water and two cloths and works from clean to dirty: the face first, then down. The barrier cream goes on before the brief, not after — she learned that the hard way, the first month, when she did it the other way around and the cream had nowhere to sit. The brief is a size small and she buys the ones with the wetness indicator because her mother cannot always tell her, and the line goes from yellow to blue when it is time. She checks the heels. She checks the sacrum. She moves the pillow that keeps the ankles from resting on each other, because two bones resting together will wear a sore the same as a bed will.

The swallow is the other thing. Her mother aspirates thin liquid now, so the water is thickened to the consistency of nectar with a powder Carol stirs in, and the pills are crushed and folded into a spoon of applesauce. There is an order to it. Crush, fold, the spoon held level, wait for the swallow before the next. If she goes too fast it comes back up.

None of this is knowledge that goes anywhere. It is knowledge about this body. She knows her mother lists to the left, so the wedge goes on the left. She knows the left hip is the tender one and to support it when she rolls her. She knows the dentures fit better if they go in wet and that her mother's eyes need the drops twice a day or they crust at the corners. When her mother dies this knowledge will be of no use to anyone. It does not transfer. It is about the one body and ends with it.

A woman from the hospice came and was kind. She said that this was sacred work, that caring for the body at the end was a way of honoring the whole life, that there was grace in it. Carol said yes because the woman meant well and there was no reason to argue. But it is not grace. It is the wash and the cream and the brief and the two cloths and the order they go in. It is being tired in a way that sleep does not fix. When she thinks about the work, which is in the doing of it and not otherwise, she is thinking about whether the heels look right or whether she ordered enough of the briefs to last the week.

Her mother does not know her some days. On those days Carol does the wash the same way. The body does not need to be recognized to need turning. It needs turning whether or not anyone is left inside it to be grateful, and Carol turns it, and the skin holds, and that is the whole of what the turning is for.

She rinses the cloths and hangs them over the rail of the tub. She writes the time of the last change on the pad by the bed so the night will know. There is a load of laundry that has to go in before she can sit down, and she goes and puts it in.
