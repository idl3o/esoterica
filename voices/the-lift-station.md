# The Lift Station

The lift station at the end of Carver Road sits below the road grade, which is the point of it. The sewage from the houses on the hill runs down by gravity and collects in a wet well twelve feet deep. Two pumps lift it back up to a main that runs to the treatment plant four miles east. When one pump runs the other rests. They alternate on a timer so they wear evenly.

He checks it twice a shift. He has checked it, or one like it, for nineteen years.

The job is mostly the wet wipes. The package says flushable. They are not flushable. They do not break apart in water the way toilet paper does; they braid together with grease and hair into a grey rope, and the rope wraps the impeller until the pump trips on high amps. Then the alarm dials his phone. He drives out, isolates the pump, cranks it up out of the well on the chain hoist, and pulls the rope of wipes off the impeller with a gloved hand. The grease is the worst part. It comes off the kitchens, congeals on the way down, and sets on everything in a layer the color of old candle wax. The smell does not come off in the shower. His wife stopped commenting on it years ago.

He is good at it. He can tell from the pitch of the motor, before the gauge moves, whether it is a wipe clog or a bad bearing. He knows the float switches are junk and orders the heavy ones now. He knows which fitting on the discharge side weeps and how long he can leave it. None of this knowledge goes anywhere. It does not transfer to anything else. It is knowledge about this station and the next one like it and that is the whole of where it lives.

Nineteen years did not make him wise. He wants to be plain about that, because people reach for it. A man came out from the city once, some kind of consultant, and stood at the rail of the wet well and said the work he did was sacred, really, when you thought about it — that he tended a threshold, that the unseen labor was what held the city up, that there was a dignity in keeping the hidden things moving. He was not unkind about it. He meant it as a compliment.

It is not that the man was lying. It is that it has nothing to do with the job. The job is the wipes and the grease and the float switches and the amps and the overtime, which he is glad to get because the overtime is how the truck got paid off. There is no threshold. There is a wet well twelve feet deep and two pumps that alternate on a timer. When he thinks about the work, which is not often, he thinks about whether the heavy floats came in yet.

His knees are worse than they were. He will do this until he can't, and then someone else will check it twice a shift, and the houses on the hill will flush and not think about it, which is correct, because there is nothing there to think about.

He signs the log. He pulls the gate shut behind him and chains it. He drives home with the windows down.

The pump runs whether or not anyone has a thought about it.
