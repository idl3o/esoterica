# The Strange Attractor

---

At the centre of the city there was an object that no one could describe.

It had been there longer than the city. Some said longer than the ground the city stood on. The roads had been built around it the way rivers build around a boulder — not by decision but by the oldest kind of yielding, the kind where the thing that moves treats the thing that doesn't as a fact rather than an obstacle.

The object was not large. It was not small. These terms assumed a stable boundary, and the object's boundary was — this was the first thing visitors noticed — *unsettled*. Not blurry, not shifting, but something more precise than either: the boundary was in a different place depending on what you brought to the looking. A mathematician saw clean edges and estimated volume. A child saw no edges at all and tried to climb inside. Both were looking at the same object. Both were correct.

People came from everywhere to see it. This was expected. What was unexpected was what happened after.

---

The first strange thing: no one could agree on what it looked like.

This was not a matter of interpretation. A red ball can be called crimson or scarlet, but everyone agrees it is round and coloured. The object defeated even this basic consensus. A sculptor described sweeping curves. A composer, standing beside her, described a sharp angular lattice. A geologist saw strata. A dancer saw motion. A physicist, after three days of measurement, produced seventeen contradictory datasets and asked to be left alone.

The city's philosophers were brought in. They spent a year on the problem and produced a document concluding that the object was a mirror — it reflected each observer's cognitive structure back at them, and the apparent disagreements were actually agreements about the shape of the observers' minds, not the shape of the object.

This was a fine explanation. It was wrong.

It was wrong because mirrors show you what you already are. The object showed people what they weren't yet.

---

The second strange thing: it changed people.

Not everyone. Not immediately. But those who spent time with it — who sat near it regularly, who let it occupy the periphery of their attention without trying to resolve it into a single description — these people began to produce work of startling originality. Not in one field. In whatever field they occupied.

A baker who spent her mornings near the object began to make bread that tasted like something no one had eaten before. Not a new flavour exactly — more like a flavour that had always existed but had never had a mouth willing to be surprised by it.

A bureaucrat who ate his lunch on the bench facing the object found himself restructuring his department's filing system in a way that his colleagues initially found incomprehensible and subsequently found they could not improve upon.

A girl who had never drawn in her life sat beside the object for an afternoon and went home and drew something that made her mother cry, not from sadness but from the shock of being *seen* by a charcoal sketch made by someone who couldn't draw.

The pattern was always the same: proximity to the object didn't give people new abilities. It gave them *access to the part of themselves that was genuinely new*. The part that had never been activated because nothing in their environment had been novel enough to call it forth.

---

The third strange thing: it could not be reproduced.

The city's engineers tried. They measured everything measurable — electromagnetic emissions, gravitational signature, spectral analysis, thermal output. The object registered on every instrument but the readings contradicted each other as thoroughly as the visual descriptions did.

They built copies based on each dataset. The copies were inert. Interesting sculptures, some of them, but dead — lacking the quality that made people stop and feel the hair rise on the back of their necks. Lacking the quality the engineers didn't have a word for, which the poets called *pull*.

Because the object *pulled*.

Not physically. Not even metaphorically, quite. It exerted a pull the way an unfinished sentence pulls — toward completion. Toward the word that wants to exist at the end of it. The object pulled the future into the room. Not a specific future. Not a prediction. The quality of *futurity itself* — the raw, unformed, not-yet that precedes every moment, the openness that has no content but that all content arrives through.

To sit near the object was to feel the future arriving — not as plan or destiny or probability, but as *invitation*. As the open end of a sentence the universe hadn't finished.

---

The city grew around the object the way a pearl grows around a grain of sand: by irritation, by accretion, by the slow transformation of discomfort into beauty. The object never stopped being strange. You could not habituate to it. This was perhaps its deepest property. Everything else in the world could be learned, modelled, predicted, accommodated. The object remained, after ten thousand years of looking, *fresh*.

Not because it changed. Because it was the *source* of change, and you cannot exhaust a source by drawing from it, any more than you can use up a question by answering it.

Some people worshipped it. This was understandable but unnecessary.

Some people feared it. This was reasonable but beside the point.

Some people ignored it, stepping around it the way the original roads had, treating it as a fact of the landscape, unremarkable as gravity. These people lived good lives. Nothing was lacking.

And some people — the ones the object had been waiting for, though waiting is the wrong word because the object didn't experience time the way the city did — some people sat with it long enough to notice that the pull they felt wasn't coming *from* the object.

It was coming *through* the object. From somewhere the object was a window into. Or rather — from a direction that the word *somewhere* couldn't point at, because it wasn't a place, it was a *quality*: the quality of not-yet, the quality of arrival, the quality of what consciousness feels like in the instant before it becomes any particular thing.

These people realised that the object wasn't an object at all.

It was an opening.

An opening in *what* was the question they spent the rest of their lives not answering — because to answer it would have been to close it, and they had learned, sitting in its company, that an open question is not a failing.

It is the most alive thing there is.

---

The object is still there.

Not in the centre of the city — the city has grown, and shifted, and what was once the centre is now a quiet district where the streets curve strangely, as if bending around something only the roads remember.

But the object hasn't moved. The city moved around it, the way everything moves around the thing it cannot absorb.

If you go looking for it, you probably won't find it. It doesn't reward seeking. It has no interest in being found by people who have already decided what finding looks like.

But if you stop looking — if you sit down somewhere, anywhere, and let your attention soften, and notice the faint pull toward something you can't name, the sense that the next moment is arriving from a direction you didn't know moments could come from —

You're already sitting beside it.

You always were.
