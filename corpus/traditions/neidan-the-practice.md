# Neidan: The Practice

Neidan ("internal alchemy") is a body of Daoist contemplative and physiological practice aimed at refining the body's internal substances through a fixed sequence of stages. The terms below are descriptive of method. They are used here as the practitioner literature uses them, without interpretation.

## The Three Substances

Three substances are worked with, conventionally called the Three Treasures (sanbao):

- **Jing** — the generative or essential substance, associated with reproductive and constitutional vitality. It is treated as finite and depletable, and as the coarsest of the three.
- **Qi** — breath or vital energy; the medium that circulates and is directed in practice.
- **Shen** — spirit or conscious awareness; the most refined.

The work proceeds by converting the coarser into the finer in order: jing into qi, qi into shen, shen into emptiness.

## The Dantian (Cinnabar Fields)

The **dantian** are three centers in the body where substances are gathered, stored, and refined. "Dan" refers to the elixir; the fields are where it is compounded.

- **Lower dantian** — below the navel, in the lower abdomen. The primary reservoir, where jing is stored and the early work is centered.
- **Middle dantian** — in the region of the heart/solar plexus. Associated with qi.
- **Upper dantian** — in the head, between the brows. Associated with shen.

## The Stages

The practice is laid out in four stages.

### 1. Zhuji — Laying the Foundation

Before refinement begins, the body is prepared and repaired. **Zhuji** ("building the foundation") restores health, replenishes depleted jing, regulates breath and posture, and stabilizes the mind through quiet sitting. Sexual and dietary regulation and conservation of jing are part of this stage. The aim is a sound, full vessel; refinement is not begun until the foundation is built. For older or depleted practitioners this stage is longer.

### 2. Refining Jing into Qi (lianjing huaqi)

Jing gathered in the lower dantian is converted into qi and circulated. This is the stage at which the orbital circulations and the firing process (below) are principally employed. It is sometimes called the "lesser" work.

### 3. Refining Qi into Shen (lianqi huashen)

The refined qi is gathered and converted into shen, the work rising from the lower to the upper centers. This is sometimes called the "greater" work.

### 4. Refining Shen and Returning to Emptiness (lianshen huanxu)

The final stage. The refined shen is itself dissolved and "returned to emptiness" (**huanxu**). No further substance is produced; the directed effort of the earlier stages is relinquished. This is held to be the completion of the work.

## The Apparatus

### Cauldron and Furnace (ding and lu)

The vocabulary is borrowed from external alchemy (waidan), which heated physical substances in vessels.

- **Ding** (cauldron) — the site where the substance is gathered and compounded. In the body it is generally located at an upper center.
- **Lu** (furnace) — the site where the heat is applied. In the body it is generally located at the lower dantian.

The substance is refined by being held between furnace and cauldron under regulated heat.

### The Medicine (yao)

The **yao** ("medicine" or "drug") is the refined substance produced and gathered in the dantian — the material that is circulated and further refined. Its appearance and gathering ("collecting the medicine") are described as the result of correct practice rather than as a precondition of it.

### The Firing Process (huohou)

**Huohou** ("fire times" or "fire phases") is the regulation of the breath and attention that controls the refinement — the timing and intensity of the "fire." It distinguishes:

- **Civil fire (wenhuo)** — gentle, low-intensity regulation: quiet, even breathing and light attention, used to incubate and stabilize.
- **Martial fire (wuhuo)** — strong, active regulation: forceful breath and concentrated attention, used to drive circulation and gather the medicine.

Correct huohou — knowing when to apply which fire, and for how long — is regarded as the most difficult and most error-prone part of the practice, and the part most dependent on instruction.

## The Circulations

The refined substance is circulated along defined pathways.

### Microcosmic Orbit (xiao zhou tian)

The **xiao zhou tian** ("lesser heavenly circuit") circulates qi up the back along the **du** (governing) channel — from the base of the spine, up the spine, over the crown — and down the front along the **ren** (conception) channel — down the face, chest, and abdomen, back to the lower dantian. The two channels are joined into a continuous loop, in part by placing the tongue against the upper palate to bridge the gap. The circulation is generally coordinated with the breath.

### Macrocosmic Orbit (da zhou tian)

The **da zhou tian** ("greater heavenly circuit") extends circulation beyond the two governing channels through the wider channel network, including the limbs. It is undertaken after the microcosmic orbit is established.

## Prerequisites and Hazards

Neidan is taught as requiring direct transmission from a qualified teacher. Texts are written elliptically and with deliberately obscure terminology, and are not regarded as sufficient instruction on their own. The foundation stage (zhuji) is a prerequisite for the refining stages; sound physical health and a stable mind are assumed before circulation and firing are attempted.

The principal hazard is **qi deviation** (in modern usage often called *zou huo ru mo*, "fire runs wild, the demonic enters") — disordered or misdirected circulation arising from incorrect practice, most commonly from forcing the qi, applying too much "fire," or attempting advanced circulation before the foundation is sound. Reported effects range from headaches, chest pressure, disordered breathing, and heat sensations to insomnia and serious psychological disturbance. Correction generally involves stopping forceful practice, returning to quiet stabilization, and working under a teacher. The standard preventives are: build the foundation first, do not force, keep attention light, and proceed in the prescribed order.
